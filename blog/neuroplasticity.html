Neuroplasticity

Research Neuroplasticity. Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.
Understanding the principles of neuroplasticity can empower people as it changes their outlook on what their possibilities are. Historically, we thought that the brain is a fixed entity and that there is little to no room for improving cognitive abilities. Recent studies have indicated otherwise and we are now beginning to understand the malleable nature of the brain. This means that our beliefs around our cognitive limitations can be challenged as the discovery of neuroplasticity has proven that we can change our brains chemically and physically.

Researchers have established 10 Principles of Neuroplasticity:

  1. Use it or lose it. Just like bodily muscles, neural pathways degrade if they are not used frequently.
  2. Use it and improve it. Training your brain with intent and focusing on enhancing specific brain functions via regular behavioural practice strengthens neural circuits responsible for those functions leading to improving targeted abilities.
  3. Specificity. Targeted training through cognitive exercises and behavioural programmes can change the physical make up of the brain and enhance the neural pathways accosiated with the training.
  4. Repetition matters. Change requires practice and regular training. This is critical especially in learning and rehabilitation.
  5. Intensity matters. How intensely you train your brain will impact the level of change in the targeted brain function, leading to a corresponding level of change in the targeted ability.
  6. Time matters. You can always make improvements because the brain is inherently malleable, however, different forms of change in the brain happen at different times during training.
  7. Salience matters. How meaningful it is for you to make this neurological change will impact the the outcomes. The more it means to you, the more motivated you are to achieve the change and the more likely you are to achieve it.
  8. Age matters. It is easier for younger people to change their brain using targeted training. This does not mean that change is impossible for older adults, it just means that there may be more time, practice, and intensity required for an adult to achieve the same outcomes as a young person.
  9. Transference. Training yourself in one task can also flow onto other similar tasks if similar neural functions are involved in both tasks.
  10. Interference. Brain changes that result in unwanted habits can interfere with learning desired habits. This is something to be mindful of.

Research Growth Mindset. Discuss what it is and why it is relevant.
A fixed mindset refers to the perception that things about you, the world, and/or others are static and cannot be changed. A fixed mindset endorses non-adaptive ideas such as, "I am bad at math, this is how it will always be, and nothing can change that." We may have linked our historical understanding about the brain being 'hard-wired' to our attitudes towards ourselves and our environment. On the contrary, we now know that the brain is capable of change due to the discovery of neuroplasticity (as discussed above). A growth mindset compliments this new discovery about the brain.

A growth mindset is the internalisation of the idea that intelligence, skills, and abilities can be enhanced and that the brain is not a static entity. Literature on growth mindsets embraces neurodevelopment research findings and encourages further development of the brain via training, repetition, and determination. Being growth-oriented also means failing, learning from failures, and pushing yourself into uncomfortable challenges that offer growth.

No-one is 100% growth-oriented. We all have things we may be fixed-minded about, while being growth oriented towards other aspects of our life. It is important to remember that life is a journey and, there is no growth if there is no room for improvement!
